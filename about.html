Our story in a snapshot
From “side hustle” to full-time calling

We started Brush & Broom LLP by helping friends and neighbors who were overwhelmed by life—kids, moves, pets, work, all happening at once.

One deep clean turned into “can you also paint this room?” and then into “can you help my sister before she lists her house?”

At some point, we realized this wasn’t just a side gig. It was the work we loved most: walking into a space that feels tired and walking out knowing we made life lighter for someone.

Our story

Built on small-town work ethic, in the middle of a busy city

We didn’t build this company with investors or fancy software. We built it with elbow grease, honest conversations, and the belief that showing up and caring still matters.

Like a lot of families, we hit a point where we were juggling everything at once—work, kids, rent increases, and trying to keep our own home from falling apart in the chaos of real life.

We saw neighbors going through the same thing. They either did everything themselves and burned out, or they hired people who were late, rushed, or treated their homes like just another job on a busy schedule.

So we asked a simple question: what if we created the kind of company we wished we could hire—one that respected people’s time, budgets, and homes as much as they do?

We cleaned our first few houses the way we would want our own cleaned. Then we started repainting trim, walls, and rentals for friends who trusted us. Word spread. One referral turned into the next, and pretty soon, weekends weren’t enough anymore.

That’s how Brush & Broom LLP was born: not from a business plan, but from real people asking for real help, and from us deciding to say “yes” and show up with everything we had.

Today, every time we walk into a home in Seattle, Kirkland, Bellevue, or anywhere on the Eastside, we remember those first clients who took a chance on us. We still work like we’re earning that trust for the very first time.

What hasn’t changed since day one

  • • We still answer our own phone.
  • • We still do the work ourselves instead of sending strangers.
  • • We still believe “good enough” isn’t actually good enough.
  • • We still measure success in texts that say “wow” and “thank you,” not just invoices.
